# Case Selection Rubric — Engineering Diplomacy

**Purpose.** Decide *which* episodes the weekly routine writes up. This replaces the
current implicit filter ("vivid, relatable, lesson generalizes") with two explicit
stages, both derived from the McNamara and Challenger reference cases and the frame
in `STYLE.md`. It's meant to be edited — the fit-test is close to objective and
mostly settled; the fertility weights are the part that should reflect Prof. Islam's
judgment about what the project is for.

---

## How it fits the pipeline

The routine brainstorms a wide candidate pool (5–8 episodes), then:

1. **Stage 1 — Fit-test (binary gate).** Each candidate must pass *all* criteria.
   Anything that fails is discarded, no scoring. This is the "is it actually an
   engineering-diplomacy case at all?" filter.
2. **Stage 2 — Fertility ranking.** Survivors are scored 1–5 on weighted criteria.
   The top two are written up in full. The next 2–3 are surfaced as one-line pitches
   at the bottom of the weekly report (the hybrid review model), so a runner-up can
   be swapped in cheaply when the top pick underwhelms.

---

## Stage 1 — Fit-test (all must pass)

Each criterion is phrased as a yes/no question, with the grounding from the seed
cases so the line is defensible.

1. **Was the analysis genuinely sound?**
   The actor must have been competent and largely *right* on their own terms — this
   is not a story about incompetence or a wrong calculation. *McNamara's body-count
   accounting was internally consistent; Boisjoly's O-ring data and reasoning were
   correct.* If the actor was simply wrong, it's an engineering failure, not an
   engineering-diplomacy failure.

2. **Did they trust a specific, internally valid model, metric, or mode?**
   There must be an identifiable thing they relied on. *McNamara: body count as a
   proxy for winning. Boisjoly: the engineer-to-engineer evidentiary mode — here is
   the data, here is the conclusion, the conversation is over.*

3. **Was there a decisive "missing column"?**
   A human, political, historical, or value dimension the model had no place for —
   *and that dimension was causally decisive to the outcome.* *McNamara had no column
   for what the Vietnamese were fighting for. Boisjoly's charts had no column for
   schedule pressure, contract relationships, or who in the room had standing to halt
   a launch.* If the missing dimension didn't actually change the result, it's a
   weaker fit.

4. **Is the failure epistemological or relational, not technical?**
   They optimized *within* a model without asking if it was the right model, or spoke
   the wrong language for the room — rather than getting the technical work wrong.
   *Precision mistaken for accuracy; the data speaking while no one positioned to act
   was listening.*

5. **Does a concrete pragmatist counterfactual exist?**
   You can name a *specific* alternative move, not just "they should have been wiser."
   *Start from "how does the other side define winning?" and bring in regional
   historians. Reframe the O-ring risk as institutional liability; identify who had
   the standing to stop the launch; escalate outside the teleconference.* If the only
   counterfactual is vague, the pedagogical payoff is thin and the case is weak.

6. **Is it real and well-documented enough to verify?**
   There must be a public record sufficient for 3–6 verifiable sources. This protects
   against fabricated sourcing and is non-negotiable for a deliverable going out under
   your name.

A useful soft signal alongside the gate: if you *cannot* find an honest "flag" — a
limitation the episode reveals that principled pragmatism itself doesn't resolve
(*ego, in McNamara; organizational power structure, in Challenger*) — the case may be
suspiciously tidy. Not an automatic disqualifier, but worth a second look.

---

## Stage 2 — Fertility ranking (score survivors 1–5, weighted)

| Criterion | Weight | What a 5 looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **Generalizability of the lesson** | 3 | The "missing column" abstracts into a transferable principle, not a one-off. *Precision ≠ accuracy; winning the technical argument isn't enough.* |
| **Dimension coverage vs. the corpus** | 3 | Exercises a missing-column type the existing cases underuse (history/motive, identity, institutions/power, values). Rewards balance; penalizes a fourth "engineer overruled by management" story. |
| **Novelty / non-canonical** | 2 | Not the case everyone already cites. The seeds are canonical *as references*; new output should generally beat them here. |
| **Counterfactual concreteness** | 2 | A specific, defensible alternative move a principled pragmatist could have taken. |
| **Cold-open vividness** | 2 | A specific person at a specific decisive moment, with date and place (per `STYLE.md` step 2). |
| **Honest-flag value** | 1 | Surfaces a *fresh* limitation of the framework, not a re-run of ego/power. These flags are the raw material for a cross-case "framework blind spots" layer. |
| **Documentation depth** | 1 | Rich enough record that the writeup is defensible and reads well. |

**Score** = Σ (criterion score × weight). Top two by score get written; next 2–3
become the runner-up pitches in the report. Ties broken toward dimension coverage,
then novelty.

---

## Validation against the two seed cases

Running McNamara and Challenger through the rubric is a sanity check (and itself
follows the paper's verify-your-tools ethos):

- Both **pass all six fit-test criteria** — expected, since the rubric is reverse-
  engineered from them.
- Both score **maximally on generalizability and counterfactual concreteness**, and
  **high on vividness** (Challenger especially: Boisjoly, Jan 27 1986, the
  teleconference).
- Both score **low on novelty** — they are the canonical cases. This is the useful
  result: it confirms the rubric will push future output *away* from re-telling
  famous cases and toward fresher ones, while still recognizing the seeds as strong
  templates.
- Their flags (ego; organizational power) are now "spent." The honest-flag criterion
  will reward cases that reveal *new* framework limits rather than re-surfacing these.

## Open questions for Prof. Islam

The weights are the editable knob — these are the decisions that should be his, not
the agent's:

1. **Weighting.** The current proposal prioritizes generalizability and corpus
   balance (3) over novelty, concreteness, and vividness (2). Is that the right
   ordering for the book's purpose, or should, say, novelty rank higher?
2. **Canonical cases.** Should famous cases be allowed at all in new output (as long
   as the angle is fresh), or excluded outright now that the seeds cover that ground?
3. **The four missing-column dimensions.** Are *history/motive, identity,
   institutions/power, values* the right taxonomy to balance coverage against, or is
   there a more native carving for the framework?
4. **The honest-flag layer.** Worth accumulating flags across cases into a recurring
   "where principled pragmatism falls short" digest? That's potentially book
   material, but it also commits the project to a stance on the framework's limits.
5. **Recency and scale.** These are currently coverage *quotas*. Under the redesign
   they become soft preferences applied after quality — is even coverage across
   regions/eras/scales a real goal, or just a nicety?
