# STYLE.md — Engineering Diplomacy case studies

## The frame
Written in the intellectual frame of Prof. Shafiqul Islam: the interplay between
**numbers and narratives, principles and pragmatism, and facts and values.**

Each case study examines a *real, well-documented* episode where a technically
competent, analytically rigorous actor — a "scientist" or "engineer" archetype —
failed. Not because their analysis was wrong, but because they treated a
fundamentally human, political, or value-laden problem as a purely technical one.
The lesson is **engineering diplomacy**: technical correctness is necessary but not
sufficient; you must navigate motive, history, identity, institutions, and power.

## The structure (follow the rhythm; do NOT print section labels)
1. **Title:** "The [Name] [Fallacy / Disaster / Concept]."
2. **Cold open:** a specific person at a specific decisive moment, with date and place.
3. **The setup:** their competence and method; the metric, model, or truth they trusted.
4. **The failure:** the gap — what their model had no column for (the human,
   political, historical, or value dimension). Frame it as an epistemological
   failure where apt: precision vs. accuracy, numbers vs. narratives, facts vs. values.
5. **The diagnosis:** a scientist optimizes *within* a model; they rarely ask whether
   it is the right model.
6. **The counterfactual:** concretely, what a principled pragmatist (an engineering
   diplomat) would have done instead.
7. **The flag:** close with a parenthetical, honest limitation — something the
   episode reveals that the principled-pragmatism framework itself does NOT fully
   address (e.g. ego, raw organizational power structures).
8. **References:** 3–6 real, verifiable sources.

## Tone & length
Narrative, precise, unsentimental. Relatable and generalizable. ~700–1000 words
(about two pages) per case.

## Reference example 1

### The McNamara Fallacy

In 1961, Robert McNamara arrived in Washington as Secretary of Defense with a
reputation as the most analytically rigorous mind in American business. He had
turned Ford Motor Company around using systems analysis, breaking complex problems
into measurable components, optimizing each one, and trusting the numbers to tell
the truth. When Kennedy brought him to the Pentagon, he brought that entire
operating philosophy with him.

Vietnam was his first real test, and he approached it the way he approached
everything: by finding the right metric. The problem with conventional warfare
indicators was that they were qualitative. Territory held, morale, political will.
These were hard to track and harder to report. Body count was clean. You could
tabulate it, chart it, brief it, and compare it week over week. If America was
killing more of the enemy than the enemy was killing Americans, America was
winning. McNamara built an entire strategy around this metric.

But the thing it measured didn't matter. The Viet Cong and North Vietnamese weren't
fighting a war of attrition. They were fighting a war of endurance, rooted in a
centuries-long history of resisting foreign occupation. They could sustain
casualties that would be catastrophic by any Western military model because the
thing they were fighting for wasn't a position on a map or a graph on a computer
system. It was something a spreadsheet has no column for. McNamara's model was
internally consistent and externally meaningless.

The failure here is an epistemological one. McNamara never asked what the Vietnamese
were actually fighting for because that question didn't fit his framework. A
scientist optimizes within a model; they rarely question whether the model is the
right one. He confused precision for accuracy. His numbers were precise. They were
measuring the wrong thing with extraordinary care.

A pragmatist in McNamara's position would have started not with "how do we measure
progress" but "how does the other side define winning?" That requires setting down
the spreadsheet and sitting with the messiness of motive, history, and identity —
none of which are quantifiable but all of which are causally decisive. It might have
meant bringing in historians of Southeast Asia, Vietnamese political analysts,
people who understood the texture of the conflict rather than its body count. The
pragmatist doesn't abandon measurement; they interrogate whether the thing being
measured is the thing that matters.

(something to flag — the US also went into the war with a lot of ego, something that
principled pragmatism does not address)

## Reference example 2

### The Challenger Disaster

On the evening of January 27, 1986, Roger Boisjoly sat in a teleconference and tried
to stop a launch. Boisjoly was a senior engineer at Morton Thiokol, the contractor
that built the solid rocket boosters for the Space Shuttle. He had spent months
documenting a problem with the O-ring seals that joined the booster segments. They
became dangerously brittle in cold weather and could fail to seal properly. He had
written internal memos flagging the risk and had the data to back it up.

That night, the forecast for the next morning's launch was 28°F — far below anything
the shuttle had ever launched in. Boisjoly and his colleagues presented their case
to NASA managers in a teleconference that lasted hours. They showed charts tracking
O-ring damage against temperature. The correlation was clear. Their recommendation
was unambiguous: do not launch.

NASA's response, delivered by a senior manager, was a line that has since become
infamous in engineering ethics: "Take off your engineering hat and put on your
management hat." Thiokol's managers, under pressure, reversed their engineers'
recommendation. The launch proceeded the next morning. Seventy-three seconds into
the flight, a seal failed, hot gas breached the booster, and the shuttle broke
apart. All seven crew members were killed.

Boisjoly was right. His data was correct, his reasoning was sound, and his
conclusion was exactly what the evidence demanded. He just wasn't equipped for a
political room. He presented the O-ring data the way an engineer presents to other
engineers: here is the evidence, here is the conclusion, the conversation should now
be over. But he was in a room where schedule pressure, contract relationships,
organizational ego, TV ratings, and NASA's public launch window all had weight that
his charts didn't account for. He treated it as a technical decision after it had
already become something else. The data was speaking. Nobody structurally positioned
to act was listening in the language he was using.

The principled pragmatist walks into that room understanding that the technical and
political conversations are happening simultaneously and that winning only the
technical argument is not enough. This might have meant reframing the risk not as an
engineering probability but as an institutional liability: if this seal fails and we
have documentation showing we flagged it and were overruled, what happens to the
program? It might have meant understanding who in that room had the standing to halt
a launch and speaking directly to their concerns rather than the group. It might have
meant escalating outside the teleconference entirely. The tragedy of Challenger is
not that no one knew. It's that it was totally preventable, yet those who did know
couldn't find the right language to communicate to those who didn't.

(something to flag — the organizational structure of NASA and who had the power
played a big role in why Boisjoly's pleas fell on deaf ears. Again, not something
principled pragmatism can properly address)
